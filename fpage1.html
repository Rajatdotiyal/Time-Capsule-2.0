The Great Pyramid of Giza in Egypt is the oldest of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, built around 2580–2560 BC.

The ancient city of Rome was founded in 753 BC.

The Battle of Marathon in 490 BC saw the Athenians defeat the Persians, securing the future of democracy.

The Maurya Empire, under Emperor Ashoka, ruled much of the Indian subcontinent from 268 to 232 BC.

The Colossus of Rhodes, another Wonder of the Ancient World, stood over 100 feet tall before being destroyed by an earthquake in 226 BC.

The fall of the Western Roman Empire occurred in 476 AD when the last Roman emperor, Romulus Augustulus, was deposed.

The Viking Age, characterized by Scandinavian exploration, trade, and raids, lasted from the late 8th to the early 11th century.

The Battle of Hastings in 1066 marked the Norman Conquest of England, led by William the Conqueror.

The Magna Carta was signed by King John of England in 1215, establishing the principle of the rule of law and limiting the monarch's power.

The Black Death, a devastating pandemic, struck Europe in the mid-14th century, killing an estimated 75-200 million people.

The Ming Dynasty ruled China from 1368 to 1644, known for maritime exploration led by Admiral Zheng He.

The Spanish conquistador Hernán Cortés conquered the Aztec Empire in 1521.

The Thirty Years' War, lasting from 1618 to 1648, was one of Europe's most destructive conflicts, involving numerous states and religious factions.

The signing of the Treaty of Westphalia in 1648 marked the end of the Thirty Years' War and the beginning of modern diplomacy.

The Enlightenment, an intellectual movement of the 17th and 18th centuries, emphasized reason, individual rights, and skepticism towards authority.

The American Declaration of Independence was adopted on July 4, 1776, leading to the formation of the United States.

The French Revolution, starting in 1789, brought an end to the absolute monarchy in France and inspired similar movements worldwide.

The Industrial Revolution, which began in Britain around the late 18th century, transformed economies and societies through technological advancements.

The Opium Wars between China and Britain took place in the mid-19th century, resulting in significant territorial concessions to the British.

The American Civil War, fought from 1861 to 1865, was a conflict between the Northern Union and Southern Confederate states over slavery and secession.

The Suez Canal, connecting the Mediterranean Sea to the Red Sea, was completed in 1869, facilitating global trade and travel.

The RMS Titanic, a British passenger liner, sank on April 15, 1912, after hitting an iceberg, resulting in over 1,500 deaths.

World War I, lasting from 1914 to 1918, involved major global powers and led to significant political and territorial changes.

The Wall Street Crash of 1929 triggered the Great Depression, a severe worldwide economic downturn lasting throughout the 1930s.

World War II, from 1939 to 1945, was the deadliest conflict in history, involving multiple nations and leading to the Holocaust.

The Cold War, a political and ideological standoff between the United States and the Soviet Union, lasted from the late 1940s to the early 1990s.

Nelson Mandela was elected as South Africa's first black president in 1994, marking the end of apartheid and promoting reconciliation.

The Berlin Wall, erected in 1961 by East Germany to separate East and West Berlin, was torn down on November 9, 1989, symbolizing the end of the Cold War.
